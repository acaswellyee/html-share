Additional Gaps — Old Taxonomy Comparison
Cross-referencing the legacy taxonomy surfaced 13 more missing categories not caught in the initial review
The legacy taxonomy organised clinical spend under department-named L2s (SURGERY, NURSING, CARDIOLOGY, LABORATORY). Comparing it against the current taxonomy revealed categories that existed in the old system — and are almost certainly present in Wellstar's AP data today — but have no equivalent node in the new structure. The Surgical Instruments gap was first discovered through a Stryker bipolar forceps test: six identical product lines scattered across four different L3 nodes because no Surgical Instruments node existed. The categories below were identified through the same lens.

What To Do With This
Option 1 — Minimum Viable
Add the 4 highest-impact nodes immediately
Surgical Instruments & Hand Tools, Perfusion & Open Heart, GI & Gastroenterology Supplies, and Patient Warming fix the largest spend gaps with the least taxonomy restructuring. All four are now included in Updated_Taxonomy_04_2026.csv.
Option 2 — Team Validation
Run the old taxonomy L2 list by Wellstar's supply chain team
Share the legacy department L2 list (SURGERY, NURSING, CARDIOLOGY, etc.) and ask: which of these do you currently track separately for budget or contract purposes? This tells you which gaps matter vs. which are theoretical — faster than guessing.
Option 3 — Data-Driven Discovery
Run AP data through the taxonomy to find catch-alls
Run 3 months of AP line items through the current taxonomy. Any concentration in Endomechanical, Other, or Nursing & General Care signals a missing node underneath. The Stryker forceps test is a good template — test more vendors the same way.
Important Note
Don't encode department in taxonomy structure
The old taxonomy used department names (SURGERY, NURSING) as L2 for visibility — but this makes AI classification harder. Keep product-type L2s for AI accuracy and add a department tag or GL-mapping layer for reporting. Trying to do both in the taxonomy is what created the forceps problem.
